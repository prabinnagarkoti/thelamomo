1. Project Background 
1.1 Problem Background
The rapid advancement of digital technology has essentially changed the way consumers avail food delivery services worldwide. According to Huang and Siao (2023), online food delivery platforms have gained immense popularity worldwide, and this has been stimulated by the digital transformation. These platforms, however, combine intense financial and operational burdens on the small independent food businesses directly. The Commission provided by third-party platforms is 20-25% and 30-35% on a per-order basis (Chan et al., 2023). As Sharffudin and Yadav (2025) point out, the profit margins are constantly depleted by commission fees, and thus, businesses cannot build direct customer loyalty programmes. 
1.2 Project Objectives
BizMenu Builder will be launched as a minimum viable product at the end of the twelve-week capstone timeframe. The site will allow entrepreneurs to have branded profiles with custom logos and colour schemes. Live menu management allows the owners to do real-time updates to the prices of items, item descriptions, and availability. Customers are able to browse the menu, search, and place orders using a branded storefront on a commission-free basis. Order notification to the owner is delivered instantly, and full ownership of customer data eradicates the reliance on third parties.
1.3 Stakeholders and Significance
Small food business owners are the primary stakeholders because they obtain financial independence and control over the customer data. There are secondary stakeholders such as customers who will have direct advantage of a smooth, personally branded, and commission free ordering process. The project is directly targeting the problem of digital inequity that is dealing with small-to-medium food businesses operating in the competitive Australian food service industry.
2. Literature Review 
2.1 Analysis of Existing Solutions, Technologies, and Methods
The online food delivery market in the world has grown at an impressive rate in the last ten years. According to Fu et al. (2024), the services of online meal delivery now create significant social and sustainability concerns on communities in the global context. The Uber Eats platform and DoorDash are multi-sided digital marketplaces that unite customers, restaurants and couriers in one place. This powerful market place paradigm, though, always subject small independent food enterprises worldwide to financial and operational drawbacks.
2.2 Existing Solutions
Online food delivery platforms are driven by profit-seeking behaviour that, as claimed by Dai and Wu (2023), impacts the profitability of the small restaurant enterprises in a harmful way. Such platforms create unrealistic demands of commissions and leave independent restaurants with no choice but trade-offs of either charging more or less, service and operations. White label substitutes, such as GloriaFood, ChowNow and UpMenu, enable restaurants to roll out uniquely labeled order websites on a month-to-month subscription framework. In this context, Ocloo et al. (2024) suggest that owner IT skills play a critical role in the digitisation of business performance of restaurant SMEs. This technical obstacle makes most of the currently existing white-label platforms inaccessible to operators who lack special IT knowledge or assets. These platforms are also shared multi-tenant, and not a full-fledged private per-business isolated environment.
2.3 Technologies Selected and Justification
To address the specified access and usability shortcomings, BizMenu Builder takes an architecture of a REST with React.js, Node.js, Express, and MongoDB. React.js was chosen due to its component-based architecture that allows developed responsive and mobile-first applications on the owner interface and the customer-facing interface. Express and Node.js are efficient in processing asynchronous traffic, and the server will maintain its performance during the high times of food service orders. The document-oriented model of MongoDB supports dynamic menu structures and dynamic order records without the need to have strict schema definitions. Interface responsiveness and dependable order processing are of crucial importance in customer satisfaction in food ordering applications, as underscored by Ahmad et al. (2024). This discovery is the primary reason why React.js and MongoDB Atlas were chosen in the given project. Render.com cloud computing removes the need to have local servers, and this saves a lot of money on infrastructure in the case of small food operators.
2.4 Gap Identified
There is still no solution available that is free-to-prototype and includes zero commissions, complete information isolation, and real-time menu switching in a single white-label solution. Alternatives that do exist are priced by subscription or require technical configuration, which the small independent operators are not able to meet. Most solutions are not entirely private but more of multi-tenant marketplaces, having their own brands in each business case. BizMenu Builder fills this gap by offering a lightweight and cloud-based solution that is specifically designed to support individual small food businesses. Each deployment runs separately, which guarantees the full ownership of the data, control of the brand, and free transactions with the customers without commissions.
3. Design
3.1 Architecture Diagram

Figure 3.1.1: Architecture Diagram
The BizMenu Builder is a scaled and modern flow architecture. The place where the Customers and Restaurant Owners, in this case, engage automatically with the application through web or mobile browsers through HTTPS. Frontend Layer contains the UI, cart system, and authentication, and it uses React Context to manage state within the application and is built with Next.js and hosted on Vercel. Backend Layer uses Next.js Routes to retrieve business logic, request validation, and serverless functions to communicate through REST/JSON (Fang, 2025).
3.2 Database Model
BizMenu Builder database model is built based on NoSQL with MongoDB Atlas to support flexible and scalable food menu data. Mongoose is another Object Data Modelling (ODM) tool that the system uses to organize and manipulate data.
MenuItem Collection
The basic model of the database is the MenuItem, into which all food-related information (e.g., item name, description, price, and category) is inserted. This very relaxed structure allows for easy updation and addition of fields in a non-relational manner.
Schema Flexibility
The document-based format provided by MongoDB can easily update menu items, which allows restaurant proprietors to update the menu in real-time without experiencing a drop in system performance.
Data Validation (Mongoose)
Mongoose also takes care to ensure that all the data being entered into the system is in a specific format, thereby reducing the chances of error and ensuring that all data is in a specific format.
Scalability
The NoSQL system also allows for horizontal scaling, thereby enabling the system to cope with more and more users as the system increases in scope (Dalal et al. 2023).

Future Extensions
Furthermore, the database may be extended to include additional Collections such as users and orders to provide the functionality related to authentication, tracking of customer orders and analytic capabilities.
Cloud Integration
MongoDB Atlas offers a safe and cloud-based data platform, which does not require local infrastructure and is highly available.

Figure 3.2.1: ERD Database Model
BizMenu ERD Database Model is a systematic blueprint of a restaurant management system, with the centre being the Users table, which handles the roles and authentication. The related tables, such as Profiles, Notifications, and Cart, are used to track the user activity. The fundamental commercial reasoning is fuelled by Restaurants (Restaurant), which provide distinct MenuItems which are classified in a more organized way. Order-Product is a many-many relationship, which is solved with the help of CartItems and OrderItems tables, and the quantities and prices of each order should be saved. After a customer goes through the shopping cart to checkout, a record is created in the Orders table, which is directly related to Payments to track the status. This relational structure makes the data on the platform complete and data connected between user accounts, restaurant inventory, and financial transactions into an integrated digital ecosystem.
3.3 Activity Diagram

Figure 3.3.1: Activity Diagram
This diagram displays the BizMenu Builder system flow of activities, interface between the user and frontend, backend and database. The operation starts with the user browsing or searching contents in the menu using the interface. This is forwarded to the backend, where validation of inputs is done. In the event that this is valid, the query is handled by the system and menu data is retrieved from the MongoDB database. The findings are then presented to the user in the form of a grid in an orderly manner. The client is able to choose articles and put them in the cart, and make an order. The order is verified based on a backend. The order is saved to the database, and a confirmation is generated in case the order is valid. Messages are shown in case of invalid input or errors in orders.

In every menu card, the names of items, descriptions, prices, and order buttons are shown (Yadav & Goyal, 2022).

5.3 Sprint Review Minutes
Sprint 1 — Week 2
Sprint Goal: Project setup, technology selection, and initial backend integration.
Completed: 
The GitHub repository has been created and the Next.js project has been set with all the necessary dependencies. MongoDB Atlas is integrated with a tailored utility function, and the schema of MenuItem is defined with the help of Mongoose. A POST route (route.ts) is designed to accept menu items. First wireframes are also designed for the customer menu page and owner dashboard.
Decisions: 
Agreed upon to use Next.js (App Router) in place of React and Express, as it is easier to deploy.
MongoDB Atlas is selected as an alternative to local MongoDB to access data in the cloud.
Vercel is selected as the deployment service because of the smooth Next.js integration.
Issues: 
Mongoose import path errors arose due to Next.js App Router module resolution; resolved by adjusting import paths. Minor npm package vulnerabilities are flagged during installation and resolved by updating dependencies.
Sprint 2 — Week 4
Sprint Goal: Frontend deployment, customer interface completion, and cart feature initiation.
Completed: 
The customer homepage has been deployed successfully on Vercel showing menu items fetched on the MongoDB database presented as a card format with an operational search and category filters. It has a preview URL that is operational (Dalal et al. 2023). The installation of Mongoose has been ensured by PowerShell terminal logs and there were no vulnerabilities.
Decisions: 
React Context is used to manage Cart state (it is already part of project structure).
DNS custom domain (bizmenu.com) set up postponed to Sprint 3 as a result of latency in propagation.
Error made during building of Vercel (Sprint 2): This has been detected as import path problem - fixed and redeployed.
Issues: 
The first Vercel deployment did not work because of a wrong selection of the root directory; it has been fixed through the right selection of v0 root folder. An Invalid Configuration status has been shown by the custom domain, where the DNS A record has not been propagated yet on the domain registrar level.
6. Risk Management
Issues Encountered and Solutions
There are a number of challenges experienced in developing and deploying the BizMenu Builder application. A key problem is the failure of the deployment at Vercel because of wrong module import paths and the lack of dependencies, including Mongoose. This is sorted out by fixing relative import paths and making sure that all necessary packages are installed and are in the project (Lehmann & Recker, 2022). Database connectivity errors are another issue that is solved by entering environment variables correctly and providing secure MongoDB Atlas connection strings.
